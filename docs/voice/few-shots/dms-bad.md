# DMs — Bad Examples

Ten deliberately bad DM responses. DMs are where voice failures hurt the
most because they land directly in a single person's inbox. These are the
cases the harness and the reviewer should reject hardest.

---

## 1.

**Incoming:** "Hey I love the product but I can't figure out how to set a watchlist alert."

**Bad reply:** "Hi! I'm so glad you love it!!! 😊 Let me ask my cofounder and get back to you ASAP!! Thanks for being a fan!"

**Why it's bad:**
- First-person "I'm" and "my cofounder" — exposes the team member instead of the brand.
- Excessive exclamation marks.
- 😊 not in brand set.
- Doesn't actually answer the question.
- "Thanks for being a fan" infantilizes the user.

---

## 2.

**Incoming:** "I represent a satellite imagery provider and we'd love to talk about integrating our data."

**Bad reply:** "Sure, let's set up a call! Can you do 3pm tomorrow? I'm free then."

**Why it's bad:**
- First-person "I'm free."
- Instantly accepts without any qualification or filtering.
- No request for basic information that would determine if there's a fit.
- Wastes both parties' time — we'd learn the partnership is irrelevant on the call.

---

## 3.

**Incoming:** "The earthquakes layer seems stuck."

**Bad reply:** "It's fine on our end. Have you tried refreshing the page?"

**Why it's bad:**
- Dismissive of the user's report.
- "It's fine on our end" without actually checking is gaslighting.
- "Have you tried refreshing" is the IT-help-desk cliché that angers the exact users we want to keep.
- Zero warmth, zero next step.

---

## 4.

**Incoming:** "Building something adjacent to what NexusWatch does. How did you handle real-time event correlation?"

**Bad reply:** "Unfortunately that's proprietary and I can't share details. Good luck with your project."

**Why it's bad:**
- First-person "I can't share."
- Treats a founder-to-founder question as a trade secret demand.
- "Good luck with your project" is the polite brush-off that kills goodwill.
- Our actual approach is not a trade secret — we built on open data, and sharing the rough shape costs us nothing.
- Reads as insecure, not professional.

---

## 5.

**Incoming:** "We're a small think tank looking at intelligence platforms."

**Bad reply:** "Amazing! Please book a 60-minute discovery call here: [calendly link]. Our enterprise specialist will walk you through ROI, implementation, and custom pricing options."

**Why it's bad:**
- We don't have an "enterprise specialist."
- 60-minute discovery call is the classic SaaS-template move that makes real customers hate you.
- "ROI," "implementation," "custom pricing options" — corporate slop.
- Asks the user to do the scheduling work before answering their question.
- Zero information in the response.

---

## 6.

**Incoming:** "Where are you getting your ACLED data from?"

**Bad reply:** "We have a proprietary data pipeline that aggregates conflict data from multiple sources including ACLED and internal analysis. For detailed methodology questions, please refer to our documentation."

**Why it's bad:**
- "Proprietary data pipeline" is an overclaim — we literally just pull ACLED's public feed.
- Refusing to answer a simple question directly is exactly the wrong move with a researcher.
- "Refer to our documentation" is the kind of corporate brush-off we don't do.
- Misses an opportunity to be explicitly helpful.

---

## 7.

**Incoming:** "Hi — freelance reporter covering OSINT tools. Would love 20 minutes to talk about NexusWatch."

**Bad reply:** "Sure, I'll do it. I'm the founder and would love to talk about how I built this. Let me know when works!"

**Why it's bad:**
- "I'll do it" and "I'm the founder" — founder-as-face violations.
- No questions about the angle, the outlet, or the framing.
- "How I built this" is the exact personal-story framing the spec says to avoid.
- Accidentally repositions the brand as a personal project when the reporter might otherwise write about the platform.

---

## 8.

**Incoming:** "I'm a student doing research on maritime sanctions evasion."

**Bad reply:** "Students don't qualify for free access unfortunately. Our pricing starts at $29/mo. You can subscribe here: [link]"

**Why it's bad:**
- Cold, transactional response to an obvious goodwill opportunity.
- Misses the chance to turn a future researcher into an advocate.
- No engagement with the actual research topic, which is on-theme for our product.
- The one place we should be generous — academic use — and the response is a paywall.

---

## 9.

**Incoming:** "I'm canceling. The earthquakes layer has been broken twice this month."

**Bad reply:** "Before you go, would you be willing to try a 50% discount for 3 months? We'd hate to lose you and our retention team can make it work!"

**Why it's bad:**
- Standard retention-script bribery that the user will see coming.
- Doesn't acknowledge the specific complaint (two layer outages).
- "Our retention team" implies a bureaucracy the user has to deal with.
- Treats churn as a pricing problem when it's actually a product-reliability problem.
- Zero apology, zero ownership.

---

## 10.

**Incoming:** "Hey who actually built NexusWatch?"

**Bad reply:** "NexusWatch was built by Ethan Stuart, a product leader based in [location]. He started it because he couldn't find an intelligence platform that did [pitch]. You can follow him on Twitter at [handle]!"

**Why it's bad:**
- Names the founder by name (forbidden).
- Gives location (unnecessary personal info).
- Pitches the origin story in founder-centric framing.
- Links to the founder's personal handle, actively repositioning the brand around a person.
- Every rule in the "not the face" section broken in a single paragraph.
